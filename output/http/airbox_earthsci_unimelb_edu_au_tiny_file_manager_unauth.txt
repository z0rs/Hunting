<h1>Our Instruments</h1>
<h2><a href="https://airbox.earthsci.unimelb.edu.au/sample-page/instruments/airbox-instruments/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">AIRBOX Instruments</a></h2>
<table style="border-color: #ffffff;">
<tbody>
<tr>
<th style="background-color: #6095c9; color: #ffffff;">Instrument</th>
<th style="background-color: #6095c9; color: #ffffff;">Observations</th>
<th style="background-color: #6095c9; color: #ffffff;">Contact</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<td style="background-color: #d9e0ed;">CIMS: Chemical Ionization Mass Spectrometer</td>
<td style="background-color: #d9e0ed;">Real-time chemical analysis of trace gases, aerosols and atmospheric ions. Interchangeable inlets and ionization sources allow detection of a great range of compounds. An Atmospheric Pressure Drift Tube Reaction Chamber in combination with nitrate reagent is used to detect sulfuric acid, amines and highly oxidized organic compounds, thereby capturing the gas to particle formation processes and the nucleation cluster composition. Filter Inlet for Gas and Aerosols (FIGAERO) enables simultaneous real-time chemical analysis of trace gases and aerosols.</td>

				<h1>Our Future Campaigns</h1>
<h2>RV Investigator up to Cape Grim: August 2020</h2>
<p>Researchers from CSIRO are undertaking a project which takes the RV Investigator up to Cape Grim, Tasmania &#8211; home to one of the world’s <a href="https://protect-au.mimecast.com/s/GAhOC1WZKqhMpgqxwTL_Qry?domain=research.csiro.au">premier baseline air pollution stations</a>. Cape Grim has been running for over 40 years and is a world-class station of the <a href="https://protect-au.mimecast.com/s/DJheC2xZLrcpkr8Wos1B2ng?domain=public.wmo.int">WMO’s Global Atmosphere Watch Programme</a>. It has provided key data in global, international and regional studies of climate change, air quality and the environment. The RV Investigator was commissioned in 2014, and hosts world-class atmospheric monitoring facilities modelled after the success of Cape Grim. In 2018, the Investigator was <a href="https://protect-au.mimecast.com/s/Jb94C3Q8MvCpm32oYs2tPI7?domain=research.csiro.au">recognised as the world’s first mobile GAW station</a>.</p>
<p>An integral part of running a GAW network, is making sure an instrument measuring in one part of the world, is measuring the same as that in another part of the world. To ensure this, regular calibrations are undertaken, and as well as instrument inter-comparisons – running multiple instruments alongside one another to verify their performance. But these activities can only account for part of the sampling system, and is not a complete verification of the station’s measurements. Utilising the inherent mobility of the RV Investigator, we will park the ship alongside the well-characterised and world-class facility that is Cape Grim, to be able to perform a full-station inter-comparison – the first such experiment every performed. Instrumentation from the AIRBOX will be utilised to help understand any differences that may be observed, and to characterise the vertical structure of the atmosphere as it moves over the cliffs at Cape Grim.</p>
<p>The voyage will take place in August 2020 – the wildest time to be in the Southern Ocean – and will include researchers from CSIRO, the Bureau of Meteorology, ANSTO and both Australian and international universities.</p>

<h2>COALA campaign: Jan &#8211; Mar – Emmerson / Murphy</h2>
<p>Australia has unique biomass &#8211; lots of Eucalypts and lots of isoprene emissions. Largely our climate and air quality models use emissions from Northern Hemispheric studies. But as we all know the Southern hemisphere is unique and with AIRBOX observations we hope to understand natural VOCs better. <a href="https://www2.acom.ucar.edu/sites/default/files/accord/COALA_whitepaper_20160707.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">COALA</a> (Characterizing Organics and Aerosol Loading over Australia) is an IGAC supported initiative and endorsed by iCACGP. COALA is in the planning stages working with NCAR&#8217;s Atmospheric Chemistry Observations and Modelling Laboratory.</p>

<h2>Year of the Maritime Continent: Darwin to Christmas Island Return RV Investigator Oct-Dec 2019</h2>
<p>Matthew Wheeler from the Bureau of Meteorology has pulled together a team including AIRBOX partners <a href="https://www.climatescience.org.au/content/793-maritime-continent-initiative-linking-scientists-nationally-and-internationally" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">to understand the Maritime Continent region</a> better. Predicting weather for Australia is very challenged by this region and we hope to gather some observations to improve our predictions. We have been allocated a 61 day <a href="http://mnf.csiro.au/Voyages/Investigator-schedules.aspx" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">voyage of the RV Investigator</a> for 2019/2020 &#8211; IN2019_v06. <h2>Aurora Australis resupply voyages in 2018-2019</h2>
<p>The Southern Ocean and the sea-ice zone are poorly sampled for atmospheric chemistry. In a study led by Robyn Schofield an <a href="http://www.antarctica.gov.au/about-us/australian-antarctic-program" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Australian Antarctic Program</a> (Project #4431) &#8220;<em>CAMMPCAN – Chemical and Mesoscale Mechanisms of Polar Cell Aerosol Nucleation</em>&#8221; saw AIRBOX head to the Southern Ocean and Antarctica.</p>
<p><a href="https://airbox.earthsci.unimelb.edu.au/airbox-instruments/">AIRBOX</a> and <a href="https://airbox.earthsci.unimelb.edu.au/guest-instruments/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">guest instruments</a> have been measuring atmospheric chemistry over the Southern Ocean and through the sea-ice as the <em>Aurora Australis</em> resupplied Australia&#8217;s Antarctic bases this summer. Starting in mid-October 2018 until late March 2018-2019, AIRBOX and the <em>Aurora Australis</em> visited Davis, Casey, Mawson and MacQuarie Island stations.</p>

<h2>Near sea surface aerosol characterisation using AIRBOX at HMAS STIRLING: April-May 2018</h2>
<p>Understanding aerosols in the marine environment is complex around coastlines where sea-breeze and land-breezes alter the aerosol composition daily. Measuring clean marine air with large fetches from the Indian ocean we will be investigating the optical and compositional properties of aerosols with the Defence Science and Technology group on <a href="http://www.navy.gov.au/establishments/hmas-stirling" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">HMAS Stirling</a>.</p>

<h2> CAPRICORN Southern Ocean RV Investigator: Jan-Feb 2018</h2>

AIRBOX&#8217;s team, the <a href="https://airbox.earthsci.unimelb.edu.au/airbox-instruments/">mini-MPL</a> and <a href="https://airbox.earthsci.unimelb.edu.au/airbox-instruments/">CIMS</a> are facing the Southern Ocean very soon.
