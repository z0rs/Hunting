				<h2>Leung Research Group at the University of Melbourne</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">We are part of the School of Chemistry and the Bio21 Molecular Science and Biotechnology Institute at the University of Melbourne.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">We conduct multidisciplinary research, drawing on the fields of chemistry, biochemistry, biophysical chemistry, enzymology, molecular biology and structural biology, to understand the complex relationships between protein structure, function and modulation. Our goal is to utilise the knowledge that we generate to help solve some of the world’s most urgent challenges.</p>
<p><strong><span style="color: #000000;"><a href="https://findanexpert.unimelb.edu.au/profile/863541-ivanhoe-leung">Find an Expert</a>&nbsp;&nbsp;|&nbsp;&nbsp;<a href="https://twitter.com/Leung_Group">X</a>&nbsp;&nbsp;|&nbsp;&nbsp;<a href="https://orcid.org/0000-0003-0633-6771">ORCID</a>&nbsp;&nbsp;|&nbsp;&nbsp;<a href="https://www.scopus.com/authid/detail.uri?authorId=6701823432">Scopus</a>&nbsp;&nbsp;|&nbsp;&nbsp;<a href="https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=TfUhMWEAAAAJ&#038;hl=en">Google Scholar</a>&nbsp;&nbsp;|&nbsp;&nbsp;<a href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=ivanhoe+leung">PubMed</a></span></strong></p>

				<h2>Our Research</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The Leung Research Group focuses on the enzymology that involves in protein function modulation. We apply a range of biophysical, chemical biology and structural biology techniques to enable our discoveries.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #094183;"><strong>Metabolic regulation of protein functions</strong></span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The ability to maintain homeostasis is critical to all forms of life. One mechanism through which this is achieved is the fine-tuning of cellular enzyme activities. In our group, we study how posttranslational modifications and metabolites affect the structure, activity and function of proteins and enzymes. These allow us to understand how different biochemical pathways signal and interact with one another to maintain stability in response to stress conditions. We are currently studying the enzymes that are involved in the regulation of carbon metabolism in <em>Mycobacterium tuberculosis</em> to understand how the pathogen survives inside the infected host with limited nutrients. Another system that we are studying is the plant ethylene-forming enzyme. We are presently investigating the mechanisms and structures of the different enzyme isoforms with a view to developing a detailed understanding of their roles in plant growth and development.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #094183;"><strong>Enzyme technology for bioremediation</strong></span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Enzymes are highly efficient catalysts that speed up chemical transformations under mild conditions. In our group, we study how we could harness the incredible catalytic prowess of enzymes as green biocatalysts to degrade environmental pollutants. We are especially interested in laccases, a class of multi-copper oxidase enzymes that catalyze one-electron oxidation of aromatic amine and phenolic substrates. Currently, we are studying how laccases mediate the degradation of organic pollutants at a molecular level to understand their mechanisms of catalysis. We also characterize the degradation products under different reaction conditions to investigate plausible degradation pathways.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #094183;"><strong>Inhibitor discovery strategies and applications</strong></span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The ability to modulate the activity of enzymes using natural or synthetic compounds has enormous potential in areas such as medicine. An important step in the early stages of inhibitor development is the identification and characterization of ligands that bind to the target. Our laboratory specializes in the application of biophysical techniques, especially NMR spectroscopy, for ligand screening and to study protein-ligand interactions and enzyme inhibition. A current interest is the development of inhibitors for lipid A phosphoethanolamine transferases. Such compounds have potential to be used as adjuvants to reverse bacterial resistance to polymyxins, a class of last-line antibiotics to treat multidrug-resistant infections.</p></p>

<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #094183;"><strong>Postdoctoral Research</strong></span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">At this time, we do not have any available postdoctoral positions. However, applicants interested in applying for their own funding are welcome to contact Dr Ivanhoe Leung for informal enquiries.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #094183;"><strong>PhD Studies</strong></span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">For those considering PhD studies, informal enquiries can be directed to Dr Ivanhoe Leung via email. The University of Melbourne has stringent entry requirements for its PhD program. Detailed information can be found on the <a href="https://study.unimelb.edu.au/find/courses/graduate/doctor-of-philosophy-science/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">University website</a>.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">PhD candidates are typically funded by the Melbourne Research Scholarship (MRS) or the Research Training Program (RTP) Scholarship. If you are a new student and have applied for a graduate research course (e.g., PhD) by the application closing date, you will be automatically considered for these scholarships. Further details about these scholarships are available on the <a href="https://scholarships.unimelb.edu.au/awards/graduate-research-scholarships" rel="noopener" target="_blank">University’s scholarship page</a>.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #094183;"><strong>MSc (Chemistry) and BSc (Honours) Studies</strong></span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">If you are interested in pursuing your Honours or Master’s research project within our group, please reach out to Dr Ivanhoe Leung via email for more information. Please note that we generally do not accept MPhil students into our research group.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #094183;"><strong>Summer Projects</strong></span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Currently, we do not have any summer research positions available. However, applicants with their own funding may contact Dr Ivanhoe Leung for informal enquiries.</p>
