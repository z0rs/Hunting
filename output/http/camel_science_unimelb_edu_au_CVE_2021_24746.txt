The genus name of the species and its common name honour the South African-born Australian scientist Ken Key, who spent his professional life studying Australian grasshoppers. Key\u2019s Matchstick Grasshopper was made world-famous as a result of evolutionary studies carried out in the 1950s and 1960s by the geneticist Michael White. White showed that different colonies of Key\u2019s Matchstick Grasshopper varied dramatically in both the number chromosomes (bundles of DNA) they had and in the way some chromosomes were assembled. He collaborated with the eminent US scientist Richard Lewontin on this grasshopper to produce text-book examples of the evolutionary process operating at the DNA level.<\/p>\n<p>White and Lewontin focused their work on cemetery populations because these remained one of the few habitats where <em>Keyacris scurra<\/em> still occurred. White noted back in 1956 that the grasshoppers were \u201cfar more widespread before settlement by the white man\u201d, but that they had been \u201clargely eliminated or greatly altered by close sheep grazing for over 100 years\u201d. He regarded the remaining cemetery populations he was studying as \u201cminute ecological islands\u201d, providing areas of \u201cone to several acres which have been fenced for many years to exclude grazing animals, and hence retain much of the native vegetation that has largely disappeared from the heavily grazed land outside\u201d. However White and Ken Key also documented the loss of the grasshoppers from cemeteries within the former range of the species, noting that of 64 cemeteries surveyed within the former range of the species, grasshoppers were only recovered in 26 of them, with many of the other cemeteries having been \u201ccleared, cultivated and burned-over\u201d.<\/p>\n<p>Both White and Key continued to survey the distribution of the grasshopper extensively in the 1950s and 1960s, leaving behind detailed field notes and scientific papers as well as specimens in the Australian National Insect Collection (ANIC). While most grasshopper are abundant in summer, <em>Keyacris scurra<\/em> hatches in mid-summer and does not quite reach maturity by the end of autumn. The species slowly develops across winter and then mates in the early spring before laying eggs, finally dying off in late spring. To find this species, you walk slowly through suitable habitat during the warmer time of the day (at temperatures above about 10 \u00b0C), and look for jumping hoppers around your feet as you disturb their favourite food plants \u2013 especially everlasting daisies (<em>Chrysocephalum<\/em>) and sheep\u2019s burr (<em>Acaena<\/em>). You then capture them with a device called a \u2018pooter\u2019 \u2013 a glass cylinder into which you can suck insects via a tube. This region is thought to contain the 17-chromosome lineage, and is located in the drier, hotter parts of the species\u2019 range. \u00a0We were very excited to find extant populations of this lineage because it may harbour genes that will help the species cope with hotter and drier conditions expected under current and future climate change.<\/p>\n<div id=\"attachment_754\" class=\"wp-caption The current drought was showing its effects here in the form of severe overgrazing by kangaroos around the town. Canberra is currently also the <a href=\"https:\/\/www.canberratimes.com.au\/story\/6189547\/canberra-named-roadkill-capital-of-australia\/\">\u2018roadkill capital of Australia\u2019<\/a>, with many kangaroos being hit as they are attracted to suburban areas during the drought where there is greener food. We visited a property in the suburb of Wamboin 16 km out of Canberra \u2013 a bush block with some high-quality Kangaroo Grass habitat and a healthy population of Key\u2019s Matchstick Grasshoppers. Two years ago, a decision was made to fence the property to exclude kangaroos because they were grazing the grass right down to the ground. Even though the grasshoppers might be able to tolerate the direct effects of the drought, they are very vulnerable to the indirect effect of overgrazing, as we failed to find them in any of the overgrazed sites. In addition to this bush block, we were able to locate Key\u2019s Matchstick Grasshoppers hanging on in bushland around Kambah Pool, and at Gibraltar Rocks in <a href=\"https:\/\/www.tidbinbilla.act.gov.au\/\">Tidbinbilla Nature Reserve<\/a> \u2013 places where they were found in a 1995 resurvey of the Canberra area for this species. And, of course, any new samples from the same sites now, over 60 years later, provide a valuable opportunity to investigate evolutionary changes over a much longer period of time. \u00a0\u00a0The site studied in the greatest detail in the 50s and 60s was Murrumbateman Cemetery, where Michael White gathered data on chromosomal changes for 11 consecutive years (1955 to 1966). We have surveyed this site now on three occasions. Unfortunately, despite a small amount of suitable habitat remaining, <em>Keyacris scurra<\/em> appears to be extinct at Murrumbateman. Many of the other old sites studied by White have suffered the same fate, including cemeteries at Wombat, Young and Cootamundra as mentioned as well as Michelago, Bredbo, Bowning, Goulburn, Yass, and Collector to name a few. White hoped to see if the Murringo chromosomes could become established but noted in one of his scientific papers that when he returned to the site the next year \u201cthe area where most of the Murringo males had been released had been ploughed up, leading to changes in the vegetation \u2026 It is recorded here in case any descendants of the Murringo males are found in future years\u201d. The total area of native grasslands that it inhabited had already contracted dramatically at the time White and Key were studying its distribution. Our survey indicates that it has since disappeared from the majority of White and Key\u2019s original sites. But it also shows that it isn\u2019t as grave a situation as it could be; the species is clinging on throughout its former range in tiny little patches as small as 0.15 hectares. We also found that a large population still lives on Memorial Hill in Goulburn, while Omeo in Victoria may also turn out to be a stronghold for the species. The Tidbinbilla population is already in a protected area and could benefit from targeted management by ACT Parks and Conservation. The population at Bredbo Cemetery south of Canberra is now extinct but it may occur in the nearby <a href=\"https:\/\/www.bushheritage.org.au\/places-we-protect\/new-south-wales\/scottsdale\">Bush Heritage Scottsdale Reserve<\/a>. Because of White\u2019s chromosome work, we have an unusually detailed understanding of the genetic diversity of this species, and our field survey suggests that the main genetic lineages persist. Genetic diversity is the key to coping with future environmental change, so we are at a critical point in time for restoring the species in a manner that gives it the best chance for the future.<\/p>\n<p>The solutions to conserving this species and its diversity are likely to be simple. In places where they still occur, we need to ensure that they are protected from overgrazing, mowing, pesticide applications and inappropriate fire regimes. Being an over-wintering species, fire management is especially important because control burns in autumn and winter could have serious effects. Captive breeding can also be used to bolster sensitive populations, and at present we are raising over 100 babies from the Hall Cemetery. Where the grasshoppers no longer occur but the vegetation is still present, fencing off areas so that they can recover from grazing should restore the habitat. \u00a0Grasshoppers can then be translocated into these areas using the most genetically suitable race. We found that many cemeteries still have kangaroo grass and native daisies, so there is great capacity for local communities to work together to resurrect these populations (even past community members can contribute by pushing up the daisies!). And in the most extreme case where the habitat has been destroyed, we may be able to reconstruct their habitat from scratch. There will almost certainly be many flow-on benefits from the restoration of Key\u2019s Matchstick Grasshoppers. This species is a very abundant food source for other animals including birds and lizards, especially in the winter and early spring when few other insects are about. We therefore expect to see an increase in biodiversity generally when the grasshoppers are restored.<\/p>\n<p>For all these reasons we think that the resurrection of Key\u2019s Matchstick Grasshopper could become one of Australia\u2019s greatest conservation success stories, as long as we act now before the remaining remnants are lost.<\/p>\n<p><strong>Acknowledgements<\/strong><\/p>\n<p>Our work In 1962 Key and White found 24 species of grasshopper here whereas we found 11 species. Despite about 4 hours of searching we only found 3 <em>W. virgo<\/em>. The population here seems to have crashed compared to previous years.<\/p>\n<p>We were excited to find three early instar <em>Keyacris <\/em>P108a, an inland version of the more well known (and now threatened in Victoria) <em>Keyacris scurra<\/em>. The <em>Keyacris<\/em> species at the Monia Gap site thus appear to have a similar life cycle but are a little behind.
